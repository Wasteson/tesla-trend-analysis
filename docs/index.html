Tesla Trend Analysis – Danish Used Car Market 2024–2026

Author

Wasteson x TrackSights

1 Objective

This analysis examines structural shifts in the Danish used car market between July 2024 and April 2026, with a specific focus on Tesla’s behavior relative to comparable electric vehicle brands.

The central questions are: Did Tesla’s listing volume and pricing dynamics diverge from peer brands during this period? And can any divergence be linked — descriptively, not causally — to the sequence of high-profile political events associated with Elon Musk and Donald Trump?

The analysis is built on weekly listing data from Bilbasen, the primary Danish used car marketplace, provided via TrackSights. All prices reflect asking prices — what sellers list, not what buyers paid.

1.1 Executive Summary

A market that moved — and a brand that moved differently

The Danish used car market expanded substantially from mid-2024 onward, with weekly listing volumes roughly doubling across all segments by early 2025. Electric vehicles led the expansion in relative terms.

Within this broader shift, Tesla stood apart — not by moving first, but by moving differently.

Compressed structural shift. Tesla’s listing volume underwent a rapid, concentrated transition between October and November 2024 — both structural breakpoints falling within approximately five weeks of each other. Peer brands such as VW ID and Skoda Enyaq showed more gradual transitions spanning months.

Price stability under volume pressure. While VW ID, Skoda Enyaq, and the broader EV market showed negative correlations between weekly listing volume and median asking price, Tesla showed no meaningful price response to volume changes — the only brand in this analysis with a near-zero or slightly positive price-volume relationship.

The Musk/Trump question. Tesla’s volume surge coincided with the period surrounding Trump’s election victory in November 2024. Price volatility patterns around the three political events examined showed brand-specific differences, most notably in the post-election period where Tesla’s volatility declined while VW ID’s remained elevated. However, with narrow event windows and confounding factors including seasonal patterns and broader market dynamics, no causal attribution is warranted.

What this means. Tesla appears to operate under a different market logic than its peers — one where supply does not drive price in the conventional sense. Whether this reflects centralized pricing strategy, brand loyalty, or sensitivity to external signals remains an open question. The pattern is visible and measurable. The mechanism is not.

2 Data & Methods

2.1 Source and scope

This analysis is based on retail listings from Bilbasen, Denmark’s primary used car marketplace, extracted via TrackSights from the table bilinfo.staging_bilinfo_single in the TrackSights BigQuery infrastructure.

Analysis period: 1 July 2024 – 3 April 2026. The start date reflects a data quality threshold: listing coverage in the source table is sparse and potentially unrepresentative before July 2024, with Tesla averaging fewer than 5 listings per week in April–June 2024. Including this period would destabilise breakpoint estimates for the early analysis window.

Data type: Flow-based analysis. The time axis reflects CreatedDate — when a listing first appeared on Bilbasen — not the total inventory at any point in time. This is an important distinction: the charts show new listings entering the market each week, not the cumulative stock of available cars. Flow data captures early signals; it does not reflect what is currently for sale.

Filters applied:

  • Retail listings only (PriceType = 'RetailPrice')
  • Minimum price: 10,000 DKK (removes leasing entries and data errors)
  • Maximum price: 99.5th percentile (~800,000 DKK) per analysis run
  • Holiday periods excluded: 20 December – 5 January in both 2024/25 and 2025/26, due to anomalously low listing activity
  • One anomalous week excluded: week of 24 August 2025, where Tesla showed 1,296 listings against a median of 120 — consistent with a batch import rather than organic market activity

2.2 Comparison group

The comparison group was selected to isolate brand-specific dynamics from distributional or structural confounders. Three criteria guided inclusion:

  1. Price segment: C/D-segment, broadly comparable new-car price to Tesla Model 3/Y (300,000–600,000 DKK)
  2. Volume threshold: Median ≥ 10 listings per week in the analysis population, ensuring reliable weekly estimates
  3. Sales model: Primarily dealer-sold (not direct-to-consumer), so that pricing differences can be attributed to brand dynamics rather than distribution structure

2.3 Methods

Weekly aggregation. Listings grouped by floor_date(CreatedDate, "week"). Weekly counts and median prices computed per segment.

LOESS smoothing. Local polynomial regression with span = 0.3, applied to weekly counts and prices. Span chosen as a balance between noise reduction and responsiveness to structural change. A sensitivity analysis with span = 0.2 and 0.5 confirms that primary conclusions are robust to bandwidth choice — see Section 5.

Segmented regression. Two-breakpoint piecewise linear model fitted to weekly volume index (week 1 = 100) per brand, using the segmented package in R. The two-breakpoint specification was chosen to capture the typical three-phase pattern observed in these data: stable period, acceleration phase, new stable level. Confidence intervals on breakpoints are reported via confint(). R² is reported for completeness but should not be interpreted in isolation — high R² is expected on trending time series and does not by itself validate model specification.

Correlation analysis. Pearson correlation between weekly listing volume and median retail price, per brand. Spearman rank correlation reported in parallel as a robustness check, given that the Pearson coefficient assumes linearity — an assumption that may not hold across the full volume range in this dataset.

Price volatility. Weekly interquartile range (IQR) of retail asking prices, used as the primary volatility measure in the event analysis. IQR is robust to outliers and does not require distributional assumptions.

3 Market context

The chart below shows smoothed weekly listing volumes for the total Danish used car market, segmented by fuel type. LOESS smoothing (span = 0.3) filters short-term noise to reveal structural direction.

Weekly listing volumes roughly doubled across all segments between July 2024 and early 2025. Electric vehicles expanded faster in relative terms, growing from a small base to represent a meaningfully larger share of total listings by mid-2025.

The drivers of this expansion cannot be fully separated from listing data alone. Three factors likely contributed: the maturation of leasing contracts signed during the 2021–2022 EV boom returning vehicles to the used market; Tesla’s repeated new-car price reductions pressuring existing owners to sell; and improving data coverage in the Bilbasen pipeline from mid-2024 onward. The relative contribution of each is not estimable from listing data.

The market-wide expansion provides necessary context for the brand analysis that follows: Tesla’s volume growth did not occur in a vacuum, and any brand-level interpretation must be assessed against this backdrop.

4.1 Smoothed listing volumes

VW ID shows the highest absolute volume and the steepest growth trajectory, particularly from late 2024 onward. Tesla and Skoda Enyaq follow more moderate paths. Hyundai Ioniq 5 and BYD operate at lower volumes throughout, consistent with their position as secondary reference brands.

Tesla’s curve is notable for two features: a delayed initial lift relative to VW ID, and a relatively flat trajectory from early 2025 onward — neither declining sharply nor continuing to grow. Whether this plateau reflects market saturation, supply constraints, or pricing dynamics is explored in Section 6.

5 Structural shifts

5.1 Breakpoint analysis

We applied segmented regression with two breakpoints to the weekly volume index for each brand. Breakpoints mark estimated inflection points where the rate of growth changed structurally. Confidence intervals are shown as shaded bands; where the CI is wide, the estimate should be interpreted with corresponding caution.

Tesla’s two breakpoints fall within approximately five weeks of each other — both in the October–November 2024 window. This compressed transition is the most analytically distinctive feature of Tesla’s volume pattern: the market shifted rapidly from low to high listing activity, then stabilised. The model fits well (R² = 0.74), though RSE = 889 reflects substantial week-to-week volatility around the trend.

VW ID shows a more gradual growth phase, with the first breakpoint around February 2025 (BP 37.9, CI [32.9–42.9]) and a second near October 2025 (BP 72, CI [41.6–102.4]). The second breakpoint carries a wide confidence interval, as it falls near the current end of the analysis period — this reflects genuine uncertainty about whether the observed flattening is a structural shift or short-term variation. R² = 0.88 with RSE = 247 indicates a stronger and more stable underlying trend than Tesla.

Skoda Enyaq shows a clear inverted-U pattern — growth through early 2025, plateau, then gradual decline. The transition between phases is spread across approximately 30 weeks, in contrast to Tesla’s 5-week compression. Hyundai Ioniq 5 shows high residual volatility (RSE = 181, R² = 0.54) — the two-breakpoint model captures the broad shape but not the week-to-week variation, consistent with the lower and more irregular listing volumes for this brand.

5.2 Timing comparison

The timing chart makes the cross-brand comparison explicit. Tesla and Hyundai Ioniq 5 both show tightly clustered breakpoints in the February–March 2025 window — their structural shifts completed quickly. Skoda Enyaq spans the longest interval between breakpoints, reflecting its more gradual market cycle. VW ID’s second breakpoint extends toward the analysis boundary with a correspondingly wide confidence interval.

Two tightly clustered breakpoints — as seen in Tesla and Hyundai Ioniq 5 — indicate a compressed transition: the market shifted from one regime to another rapidly. This is qualitatively different from Skoda Enyaq’s gradual multi-month transition, and may reflect different sensitivities to external signals, supply shocks, or brand-specific demand dynamics.

6 Price dynamics

6.1 Volume–price correlation

The chart shows Pearson r (circles) and Spearman ρ (triangles) for each brand. Both methods agree in direction across all segments — the lineariy assumption embedded in Pearson does not materially affect the conclusions here.

All brands except Tesla show significant negative correlations between weekly listing volume and median asking price: more listings correspond to lower prices. This is consistent with standard supply-driven price dynamics.

Tesla is the exception. Both Pearson (r = 0.06, p = 0.57) and Spearman (ρ = -0.19, p = 0.07) are near zero and non-significant. Tesla’s asking prices do not respond to supply in the way observed for any other brand in this analysis.

Each point represents one week. The linear trendline per brand visualises the direction and magnitude of the volume–price relationship.

6.3 Smoothed price trends

LOESS-smoothed median asking prices reveal the structural price direction over time. Fossil vehicles show a consistent decline throughout the period. Electric vehicles follow a similar path with more variation. VW ID tracks the EV average closely. Skoda Enyaq starts highest among EV brands and shows the steepest decline toward the end of the period.

Tesla maintains the most stable price trajectory of any EV brand — neither declining sharply nor showing the volatility of Skoda Enyaq or Hyundai Ioniq 5. Combined with the correlation findings, this supports the interpretation that Tesla’s asking prices are managed differently — whether through centralized pricing strategy, stronger brand loyalty among sellers, or a combination of both.

7 The Musk/Trump question

7.1 Hypothesis and operationalisation

Tesla’s compressed volume surge occurred in October–November 2024 — a period that coincides with Trump’s election victory on November 5, 2024. This raises a testable question: did Tesla’s market behavior show brand-specific sensitivity to the political events associated with Elon Musk and Donald Trump?

We operationalise this as a volatility hypothesis: Tesla’s weekly asking-price IQR increased in the weeks surrounding the three political events, relative to (a) Tesla’s own pre-event baseline and (b) VW ID and Hyundai Ioniq 5 in the same weeks.

Price volatility — measured as the weekly interquartile range of retail asking prices — is used rather than median price, as it captures market uncertainty and heterogeneity more directly than central tendency.

7.2 Volatility over time

Tesla’s IQR is elevated in the early period and declines gradually through 2025. VW ID’s IQR increases substantially over the same period — reflecting the growing heterogeneity of a market with increasing volumes and a wider range of model variants and conditions. Hyundai Ioniq 5 remains consistently lower, consistent with its narrower volume base.

7.3 Event windows

In the ±4-week window around Musk’s Trump endorsement (July 13, 2024), Tesla and Hyundai Ioniq 5 both show near-zero IQR on the event week itself. This should be interpreted with caution: Tesla averaged fewer than 10 listings per week in July 2024, meaning IQR estimates are highly sensitive to individual listings and do not constitute reliable signal.

The election window is the most informative of the three. Tesla and VW ID move in parallel through October, both peaking in the week of the election. After the election, the two brands diverge: VW ID’s IQR remains elevated through November and December, while Tesla’s declines back to pre-election levels. This asymmetric post-election pattern is consistent with the interpretation that VW ID’s market faced greater ongoing uncertainty — plausibly reflecting concerns about trade policy and European automotive exposure — while Tesla’s market normalised more rapidly.

This divergence is the closest the data come to supporting the brand-specific hypothesis, but it falls short of confirmation. The pattern could equally reflect seasonal dynamics, model mix changes, or the natural stabilisation of Tesla’s market following its compressed growth phase.

Around the inauguration (January 20, 2025), Tesla’s IQR declines consistently while VW ID rises modestly. Hyundai Ioniq 5 shows a spike immediately before the event date followed by a sharp drop — a pattern likely driven by low volume rather than market signal. No clear Tesla-specific response is visible here.

7.4 Assessment

Across the three events, the volatility data do not provide consistent support for the hypothesis that Tesla’s asking-price behavior was distinctively sensitive to Musk/Trump-associated events. The election window shows the most interesting divergence, but with only 9 weekly observations per brand in each event window and several plausible confounding explanations, the evidence is suggestive rather than conclusive.

The more robust finding from the volume analysis — Tesla’s compressed structural shift in October–November 2024 coinciding with the election period — remains the stronger signal. But coincidence in timing is not mechanism, and listing data alone cannot resolve whether the surge was driven by sentiment, supply dynamics, or factors unrelated to the political context.

8 Final takeaway

Tesla’s behavior in the Danish used car market during 2024–2026 is analytically distinct from its peers across multiple dimensions: a compressed structural volume shift, stable asking prices under supply pressure, and a near-zero price-volume correlation that stands alone among the brands examined.

What this pattern does not tell us is why. Centralized pricing strategy, brand loyalty among sellers, or genuine market sentiment around the Tesla brand are all consistent with the data. Causal attribution requires a credible identification strategy that listing data cannot provide.

What the data can establish is that the pattern is real, measurable, and consistent across methods. That is the appropriate scope of this analysis — and it is sufficient to make the finding worth reporting.

The pattern is visible and robust. The mechanism remains an open question — and an honest one.

# Price dynamics

# The Musk/Trump question

## Hypothesis and operationalisation

Tesla's compressed volume surge occurred in October–November 2024 —
a period that coincides with Trump's election victory on November 5,
2024. This raises a testable question: did Tesla's market behavior
show brand-specific sensitivity to the political events associated
with Elon Musk and Donald Trump?

We operationalise this as a volatility hypothesis: *Tesla's weekly
asking-price IQR increased in the weeks surrounding the three
political events, relative to (a) Tesla's own pre-event baseline
and (b) VW ID and Hyundai Ioniq 5 in the same weeks.*

Price volatility — measured as the weekly interquartile range of
retail asking prices — is used rather than median price, as it
captures market uncertainty and heterogeneity more directly than
central tendency.

## Volatility over time
```{r}
include_graphics("output/09_volatility_iqr.png")
```

Tesla's IQR is elevated in the early period and declines gradually
through 2025. VW ID's IQR increases substantially over the same
period — reflecting the growing heterogeneity of a market with
increasing volumes and a wider range of model variants and conditions.
Hyundai Ioniq 5 remains consistently lower, consistent with its
narrower volume base.

## Event windows
```{r}
include_graphics("output/10_event_volatility_musk_backs_trump.png")
```

In the ±4-week window around Musk's Trump endorsement (July 13,
2024), Tesla and Hyundai Ioniq 5 both show near-zero IQR on the
event week itself. This should be interpreted with caution: Tesla
averaged fewer than 10 listings per week in July 2024, meaning
IQR estimates are highly sensitive to individual listings and do
not constitute reliable signal.
```{r}
include_graphics("output/10_event_volatility_trump_elected.png")
```

The election window is the most informative of the three. Tesla and
VW ID move in parallel through October, both peaking in the week
of the election. After the election, the two brands diverge: VW ID's
IQR remains elevated through November and December, while Tesla's
declines back to pre-election levels. This asymmetric post-election
pattern is consistent with the interpretation that VW ID's market
faced greater ongoing uncertainty — plausibly reflecting concerns
about trade policy and European automotive exposure — while Tesla's
market normalised more rapidly.

This divergence is the closest the data come to supporting the
brand-specific hypothesis, but it falls short of confirmation.
The pattern could equally reflect seasonal dynamics, model mix
changes, or the natural stabilisation of Tesla's market following
its compressed growth phase.
```{r}
include_graphics("output/10_event_volatility_trump_inaugurated.png")
```

Around the inauguration (January 20, 2025), Tesla's IQR declines
consistently while VW ID rises modestly. Hyundai Ioniq 5 shows a
spike immediately before the event date followed by a sharp drop —
a pattern likely driven by low volume rather than market signal.
No clear Tesla-specific response is visible here.

## Assessment

Across the three events, the volatility data do not provide
consistent support for the hypothesis that Tesla's asking-price
behavior was distinctively sensitive to Musk/Trump-associated events.
The election window shows the most interesting divergence, but with
only 9 weekly observations per brand in each event window and
several plausible confounding explanations, the evidence is
suggestive rather than conclusive.

The more robust finding from the volume analysis — Tesla's compressed
structural shift in October–November 2024 coinciding with the
election period — remains the stronger signal. But coincidence in
timing is not mechanism, and listing data alone cannot resolve
whether the surge was driven by sentiment, supply dynamics, or
factors unrelated to the political context.

---

# Final takeaway

Tesla's behavior in the Danish used car market during 2024–2026
is analytically distinct from its peers across multiple dimensions:
a compressed structural volume shift, stable asking prices under
supply pressure, and a near-zero price-volume correlation that stands
alone among the brands examined.

What this pattern does not tell us is *why*. Centralized pricing
strategy, brand loyalty among sellers, or genuine market sentiment
around the Tesla brand are all consistent with the data. Causal
attribution requires a credible identification strategy that listing
data cannot provide.

What the data can establish is that the pattern is real, measurable,
and consistent across methods. That is the appropriate scope of this
analysis — and it is sufficient to make the finding worth reporting.

> *The pattern is visible and robust. The mechanism remains an open
> question — and an honest one.*

## Method notes

**Data source:** `imposing-yen-426717-u4.bilinfo.staging_bilinfo_single`
via TrackSights BigQuery infrastructure. Listing creation date
(`CreatedDate`) used as time axis throughout.

**Asking prices only.** All price figures reflect retail asking
prices — what sellers list on Bilbasen. Transaction prices are not
available in this dataset. Asking prices are a proxy for market
signals, not a record of what buyers paid.

**Flow, not stock.** Weekly counts reflect new listings entering the
market, not total inventory. A spike in weekly flow means more
sellers listed that week — not necessarily that more cars were
available overall.

**Holiday exclusion.** December 20 – January 5 excluded in both
2024/25 and 2025/26 due to anomalously low and unrepresentative
listing activity.

**Anomaly exclusion.** Week of August 24, 2025 excluded from Tesla
segment (1,296 listings vs. median 120) — consistent with a batch
import rather than organic market activity.

**LOESS bandwidth.** span = 0.3 throughout. Sensitivity analysis
confirms conclusions are robust to span = 0.2 and 0.5.

**Segmented regression.** `segmented` package, R. Two breakpoints
per brand. Confidence intervals via `confint()`. Start values
psi = c(15, 40).

**Correlation.** Pearson r and Spearman ρ reported in parallel.
p-values based on two-sided t-test (Pearson) and asymptotic
approximation (Spearman).

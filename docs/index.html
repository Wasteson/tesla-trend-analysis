Executive Summary

A market that moved — and a brand that moved differently

Tesla stood apart across multiple dimensions

The Danish used car market expanded substantially from mid-2024 onward, with weekly listing volumes roughly doubling across all segments by early 2025. Electric vehicles led the expansion in relative terms.

Compressed structural shift. Tesla's listing volume underwent a rapid, concentrated transition in October–November 2024 — both structural breakpoints falling within approximately five weeks of each other. Peer brands showed more gradual transitions spanning months.

Price stability under volume pressure. All brands except Tesla showed negative correlations between weekly listing volume and median asking price — meaning prices fell as more listings entered the market. Tesla showed no meaningful price response to volume changes.

The Musk/Trump question. H2 2024 was a Tesla-specific period of elevated price disagreement among sellers — not seen in VW ID or Skoda Enyaq in the same window. The pattern is consistent with the period in which Elon Musk became an increasingly prominent political figure. The explanation remains open.

Market context

The broader market

Total market expansion — Electric vehicles led in relative terms

Weekly listing volumes roughly doubled across all segments between July 2024 and early 2025. Two factors likely contributed: the maturation of leasing contracts signed during the 2021–2022 EV boom returning vehicles to the used market, and Tesla's repeated new-car price reductions putting pressure on existing owners to sell. How much each factor contributed cannot be determined from listing data alone.

The market-wide expansion provides necessary context for the brand analysis that follows: Tesla's structural shift did not occur in a vacuum.

Brand dynamics — volume

Brand comparison

VW ID leads in volume — Tesla and Skoda Enyaq track closely

VW ID leads in volume throughout the period — roughly three times the weekly listings of Tesla and Skoda Enyaq, which track each other closely. BYD and Hyundai Ioniq 5 operate at volumes too low to draw conclusions from their curve shapes.

All three larger brands show a similar broad pattern: growth through autumn 2024, a pause around the turn of the year, renewed growth into early 2025, and a gradual settling thereafter. The December pause is consistent with seasonal listing behaviour rather than a response to any specific event. VW ID's growth curve starts earlier and is steeper, but the overall shape is shared across brands. Tesla is not meaningfully different from Skoda Enyaq in either timing or trajectory at this level of analysis.

Structural shifts

Breakpoint analysis

How brands shifted — compressed vs. gradual

While the volume chart above shows that brands follow a similar broad shape, the speed of structural transition differs markedly. Segmented regression with two breakpoints reveals where each brand's rate of growth changed structurally — and how quickly that transition happened.

A confidence interval (CI) reflects the range of plausible values for the breakpoint — a narrow band means the timing is estimated precisely; a wide band means it could plausibly have occurred across a broader window. R² measures how well the model tracks the actual weekly data — closer to 1 means a tighter fit.

Tesla's two breakpoints cluster tightly in October–November 2024 (weeks 15 and 20 from July 2024) — the market shifted rapidly from low to high listing activity, then stabilised. Hyundai Ioniq 5 also shows a compressed transition, but later — March–April 2025. Skoda Enyaq spans the longest interval, reflecting a gradual cycle. VW ID's second breakpoint extends toward the analysis boundary with a wide CI.

Price dynamics

Overview

Three lenses on the same question: does supply drive price?

The three charts below approach the same question from different angles. The correlation chart shows the statistical relationship between weekly listing volume and median price per brand. The scatter plot makes that relationship visible at the individual week level. The LOESS price chart shows how median asking prices evolved over time. Together they point to the same finding: Tesla's asking prices behave differently from every other brand in this analysis.

Volume–price correlation

Tesla is the only brand where prices do not fall as supply increases

All brands except Tesla show significant negative correlations between weekly listing volume and median asking price — meaning prices fell as more listings entered the market. This is consistent with standard supply-driven price dynamics.

Tesla is the exception — the only brand with a near-zero, non-significant correlation. Hyundai Ioniq 5 sits between Tesla and the other brands — more moderate than VW ID and Skoda, but not near zero like Tesla.

Note on Hyundai Ioniq 5 in the scatter chart: the steep negative slope is partly a visual artefact of its narrow volume range (0–100 listings per week). The Pearson and Spearman coefficients tell a more moderate story.

The Musk/Trump question

Hypothesis

Did Tesla's market show signs of unusual instability around the political events?

Tesla's compressed volume surge occurred in October–November 2024 — coinciding with Trump's election victory on November 5, 2024. We look at price volatility — specifically how widely asking prices were spread from week to week — as a proxy for market uncertainty. The IQR (interquartile range) is the distance between the 25th and 75th percentile of prices in a given week: a wide IQR means prices were spread across a large range; a narrow IQR means most listings were priced similarly. A calm market has sellers pricing similarly. An uncertain market has sellers pricing across a wide range.

A Tesla-specific pattern in H2 2024

Sellers were uncertain about what a Tesla was worth

Tesla's price spread rose sharply from July 2024 — the steepest increase of any brand in the chart — peaking around the time of the US election and inauguration. VW ID and Skoda Enyaq were relatively stable through the same period. From early 2025, Tesla's spread declined gradually while VW ID and Skoda rose. By late 2025 all three brands had converged to similar levels.

This pattern is visible in the raw weekly prices as well — Tesla showed markedly larger price swings than peers throughout H2 2024. The simplest interpretation is that sellers were uncertain about what a Tesla was worth during this period. The uncertainty built from July 2024, peaked around the election and inauguration, and resolved gradually through 2025.

This is consistent with the period in which Elon Musk became an increasingly prominent political figure — but it is equally consistent with the dynamics of a rapidly growing market attracting a wider mix of cars and sellers. The data cannot separate these explanations.

Across all three events, Tesla and VW ID moved in opposite directions. Tesla's price spread was widest around Musk's Trump endorsement in July 2024 — the earliest signal — and narrowed at both the election and inauguration. One reading: the uncertainty peaked when Musk entered the political arena, and resolved gradually as the political outcome became clear. VW ID followed the opposite path — stable in July, wider at the election and inauguration — consistent with a market absorbing uncertainty about European trade exposure rather than brand-specific sentiment. The data cannot confirm either interpretation. But the divergence is consistent across all three windows and the two directions tell different stories.

Assessment

A real and temporally structured pattern — mechanism remains open

The volatility data point to H2 2024 as a Tesla-specific period of elevated price disagreement among sellers — a pattern not seen in VW ID or Skoda Enyaq in the same window, and one that resolved gradually through 2025. This is the closest the data come to a Musk/Trump signal. The timing is consistent with Musk's entry into the political arena in July 2024 and the subsequent election period. But timing alone is not mechanism — the same pattern could reflect the dynamics of a rapidly expanding market. What the data establish is that Tesla behaved differently from its peers during this specific period across two independent measures. The pattern is real and temporally structured. The explanation remains open.

Final takeaway

Conclusion

The pattern is visible and robust. The mechanism remains an open question.

Tesla's behavior in the Danish used car market during 2024–2026 is analytically distinct from its peers — and that distinction is real, measurable, and consistent across methods. That is the appropriate scope of this analysis.
